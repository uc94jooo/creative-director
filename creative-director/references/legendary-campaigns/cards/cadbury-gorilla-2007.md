# Gorilla (Cadbury · 2007)

## Insight
Cadbury had a crisis: a salmonella contamination scandal had severely damaged brand trust. Every PR and advertising instinct said: apologize, reassure, demonstrate quality. But Fallon understood that trust is rebuilt emotionally, not rationally. Cadbury's emotional brand territory was joy — pure, irrational, effortless joy.

## Mechanic
A gorilla sits behind a drumkit. Phil Collins' "In the Air Tonight" builds. The gorilla drums the iconic fill with total commitment and visible pleasure. No product, no logo until the final frame. Duration: 90 seconds.

## Why it worked
Three patterns intersect at maximum efficiency. P11 absurdism: a gorilla playing drums is preposterous and inexplicable, which makes it impossible to look away. P16 long-form drama: the entire 90 seconds is structured as tension-build-release, with the drum fill as climax — earned, not given. P07 invisible brand: Cadbury appears only at the end, trusting that the emotional transfer from gorilla-joy to brand is automatic. The spot restored Cadbury's brand likeability faster than any apology campaign could have. The viral spread predated YouTube's advertising capability — it spread because people wanted to share it, not because it was promoted.

## Steal
When a brand needs emotional rehabilitation: do not address the crisis. Address the emotional territory the brand owns. Pure, unexplained joy demonstrates confidence; apology demonstrates guilt. The gorilla didn't say "we're sorry" — it said "we're still Cadbury." The 90-second runtime was essential: shorter would have lost the tension; any copy would have broken the spell.
