# Mars Blackmon (Nike Jordan · 1988)

## Insight

Michael Jordan was culturally real in Black America in a way that standard celebrity endorsement could not capture. Spike Lee's Mars Blackmon character (from "She's Gotta Have It") was also culturally real — hyperbolically devoted to Jordan, speaking in the authentic voice of Nike's actual audience rather than aspirational lifestyle copy.

## Mechanic

A TV campaign series: Spike Lee's Mars Blackmon character (obsessed Jordan fan) appears with Michael Jordan. Mars insists that "the secret" to Jordan's abilities is the shoes — Jordan repeatedly denies it. The comedy rests on the absurdity of the claim and on the authentic chemistry between Lee and Jordan. "Is it the shoes?" became a cultural catchphrase. The campaign ran for seven years.

## Why it worked

P11 (absurd as carrier): the premise (shoes as superpower) is transparently absurd, and the campaign made the absurdity the joke rather than trying to sell it straight. P16 (long-form drama): the ongoing dynamic between Mars and Jordan developed across episodes like a sitcom, building genuine audience investment. Nike simultaneously acknowledged and satirized the mythology of the Air Jordan — a rare creative move that made both the brand and the audience more sophisticated.

## Steal

When your product has been mythologized by its own fans, bring the fan's voice into the campaign. Cast the believer, not the athlete. The fan's hyperbole is more believable than the athlete's endorsement because the audience already knows why the fan believes it.
