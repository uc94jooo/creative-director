# Dream Crazy (Nike · 2018)

## Insight
In a culture of manufactured inspiration, the most radical thing a brand can do is put its name and money behind someone the establishment has punished for their beliefs. Colin Kaepernick was not a risk — he was a proof of brand conviction in the only moment that conviction could be tested.

## Mechanic
Nike launched a 90-second film narrated by Kaepernick — whose NFL career was effectively ended for kneeling during the national anthem — featuring athletes including LeBron James, Serena Williams, and Paralympic athletes. The tagline: "Believe in something. Even if it means sacrificing everything." The film was released on the 30th anniversary of "Just Do It." Nike stock dropped 2% in one day; it rose 36% over the following year.

## Why it worked
P04 (brand as activist) executed at maximum cost — Nike did not choose a safe issue but took a side in an active national controversy. P13 (truth-telling) named what the culture was avoiding: that protesting injustice had become a punishable act. The brand's sacrifice (stock price, consumer boycotts) was the proof that the message was genuine. No campaign in this era more precisely demonstrated the Kaepernick-as-brand-value equation.

## Steal
A brand's willingness to lose something is its credibility. If the social position costs nothing, it signals nothing. Find the issue where your brand's existing customers will push back — and ask whether your conviction is strong enough to absorb that pressure. If it is, the act becomes the brand.
