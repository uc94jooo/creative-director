# Brilliant Minds — 100th Edition (Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung · 2024)

## Insight
Germany's most prestigious newspaper has run 99 editions of a campaign featuring cultural icons with faces hidden by the paper — Oscar winners, Nobel laureates, chancellors. The 100th edition demanded a choice that would say something about Germany right now, as right-wing extremism was rising. The most powerful brilliant mind is not the most famous one, but the one who has most reason to have given up on the country and hasn't.

## Mechanic
For the 100th edition of the Brilliant Minds campaign, FAZ chose Margot Friedländer — at 102 years old, one of Germany's last living Holocaust survivors, who had lost her entire family at Auschwitz. She appeared in the iconic format: face hidden by the newspaper, identity revealed only by surroundings. The campaign ran against the backdrop of growing far-right political movements in Germany, making the subject selection an implicit political statement without a single word of political commentary.

## Why it worked
P03 (Truth Bomb): choosing a Holocaust survivor for the 100th edition says something Germany needed to hear without saying it directly — the country's most brilliant minds include those who survived its darkest chapter and chose to remain. P13 (Time or Scale): 99 editions of accumulated prestige made the 100th edition carry the weight of a century of cultural significance; the scale of the series transformed a single portrait into a statement. The choice was self-evidently right in a way no amount of explanation could improve, which is the hallmark of great editorial judgment in advertising.

## Steal
When a campaign reaches a milestone number, use the scale accumulated to say something the earlier editions couldn't. The 100th entry in any series carries institutional weight; spend it on the most morally unambiguous choice available, not the most famous one. Prestige earned through consistency can be deployed as political courage without appearing political.
