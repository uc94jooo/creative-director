# Just Do It (Nike · 1988)

## Insight

In 1987, Nike was losing to Reebok — a brand that had captured the aerobics boom. Nike's core was performance athletes; Reebok owned casual consumers. Dan Wieden's insight was not about athletes at all — it was about the moment before you start. The internal negotiation everyone faces before exercise, before any difficult commitment. "Just Do It" is not a brand claim; it is the voice in your head that wins the argument every time.

## Mechanic

The first TV spot featured 80-year-old Walt Stack running across the Golden Gate Bridge every morning — ordinary, unglamorous, undeniable. The tagline "Just Do It" appeared at the end. The campaign ran for decades, encompassing elite athletes, non-athletes, cultural icons, and ordinary people — all animated by the same imperative. It remains active 37 years later. Nike grew from $877 million to $9.2 billion in revenue in the decade following its launch.

## Why it worked

P13 (truth telling): the insight named something universally felt but never articulated by a brand — the moment of resistance before action. P16 (long-form drama) allowed individual films to be genuinely cinematic, earning attention rather than demanding it. P06 (limitation as power): Nike's limitation in 1988 was that it was "only" for serious athletes — "Just Do It" reframed that as the brand's core strength by defining the serious-athlete mindset as something everyone contains.

## Steal

The best taglines name an internal emotional state that the audience already recognizes but has never heard spoken aloud. If the brand can own that phrase — the one that plays in your head at the key moment — the brand lives in a space no media buy can create.
