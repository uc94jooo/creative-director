# Thank You, Mom (P&G · 2012)

## Insight
At the Olympics, the athlete is always the story. But behind every Olympic athlete is a parent — almost always a mother — who drove them to practice at 5am, sacrificed financially, and believed when others didn't. P&G makes products that help run homes. The real P&G hero was already there; no one had framed the story correctly.

## Mechanic
A series of long-form films showing Olympic athletes from their childhood through the games — always framed through the mother's perspective. The athlete's achievement is the backdrop; the mother's journey is the story. "It takes someone who believes in you, so you can believe in yourself."

## Why it worked
P&G didn't claim athletic performance — it claimed the emotional infrastructure of athletic performance. The role of domestic products in enabling that infrastructure is genuine: laundry, household cleaning, personal care. The campaign is not a metaphor; it's a direct claim about what P&G actually does. The emotional power is proportional to the truth of the insight.

## Steal
When the product's role is supporting rather than starring, find the story where supporting is the heroic act. The unglamorous logistics of care — what mothers actually do — are more emotionally powerful than any aspirational narrative precisely because they are real and underappreciated.
