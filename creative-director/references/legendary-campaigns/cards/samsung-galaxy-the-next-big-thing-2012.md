# The Next Big Thing (Samsung Galaxy · 2012)

## Insight
Apple fans queued for hours to buy the iPhone while the new Samsung Galaxy — objectively more feature-rich — went unnoticed. The insight: Apple users are not buying technology; they are buying status and identity. And that identity is vulnerable to being made to look absurd by someone who just walked past them in the queue.

## Mechanic
A series of spots set outside Apple stores during iPhone launch queues. Samsung Galaxy users walk past the queue, using features that iPhone users do not have — NFC sharing, larger screens, better cameras. iPhone queuers notice and become envious or dismissive, defending their wait with increasingly implausible logic. "I'm holding a place for my mom." The humor comes from the queuers' denial. Galaxy users are portrayed as casual, amused, unconcerned with status.

## Why it worked
P02 (enemy or conflict): the enemy is not Apple itself — it is the culture of brand devotion that makes consumers wait outside stores in the cold for a phone. P16 (long-form drama): each spot is a small social comedy with character arcs. The campaign's genius is that it attacked Apple culture, not Apple products, which meant it could not be defended by specifications — only by embarrassment. Widely credited with shifting Samsung's brand position in the US market.

## Steal
When your competitor has cultlike loyalty, attack the cult, not the product. Product comparisons are defensible; cultural critique of brand devotion is not. The iPhone queue is not a product feature — it is a behavior, and behaviors can be made to look ridiculous.
