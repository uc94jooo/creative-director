# Still Free (Ecko Unlimited · 2006)

## Insight
In a culture obsessed with official gatekeeping and brand control, street credibility is more valuable when it proves the establishment can't stop you. Tagging Air Force One — even via video — said everything about where Ecko stood.

## Mechanic
A viral video depicting someone spray-painting a giant Ecko logo on Air Force One. The footage was made to look authentic enough that the Pentagon was forced to publicly deny it, generating massive earned media. Users spread the video believing it was real; the brand never formally confirmed or denied.

## Why it worked
The denial from the Pentagon became the campaign. Ecko didn't need to buy media — the government's response proved the brand's anti-establishment positioning more credibly than any ad could. Tech-as-canvas (the viral video format of 2006) was used not as a distribution channel but as a credibility mechanism.

## Steal
When your brand's claim is defiance, the most powerful proof is an authority figure trying to shut you down. Design provocations that invite official denial — the denial is the amplification.
