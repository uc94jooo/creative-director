# Got Milk? (California Milk Processor Board · 1993)

## Insight
Nobody thinks about milk until they don't have it. The insight is deprivation, not benefit — milk's value is only understood in its absence. Every positive claim about milk was already known and ignored; the only fresh angle was the moment of desperate need when the carton is empty.

## Mechanic
A campaign built around deprivation scenarios — the most famous a TV spot showing an obsessive Aaron Burr historian eating a peanut butter sandwich when the radio calls for the answer to a trivia question worth $10,000: "Who shot Alexander Hamilton?" He knows the answer but cannot speak through the peanut butter — he has no milk. Tagline: "Got milk?" The two-word question became one of the most parodied and licensed taglines in advertising history. Extended into a decade of print ads with celebrities wearing milk mustaches.

## Why it worked
P09 (user as co-author): the tagline became a cultural template — "Got [X]?" entered the vernacular and was adopted by thousands of unrelated contexts, extending the campaign's reach infinitely beyond its paid media. P10 (serialization): the milk mustache print campaign ran for 20+ years with rotating celebrities, creating annual media events around each new execution. The deprivation insight was inexhaustible — any situation where milk would be needed could produce a new execution.

## Steal
If the positive benefits of your product are already known and dismissed, try communicating through the negative — the moment of absence, the experience of not having it. Loss aversion is stronger than gain motivation. "Got Milk?" does not tell you why milk is good; it shows you why its absence is catastrophic.
