# He Said She Said (The New York Times · 2018)

## Insight
During the height of #MeToo, conflicting testimonies about sexual misconduct were systematically framed as a matter of "he said, she said" — as if two accounts were automatically equal. Journalism's job is to report what it found, not to split the difference.

## Mechanic
A Twitter thread/social post in which The Times restated, side by side, what the accused said and what the evidence showed — demonstrating that the "he said, she said" frame was false when one side was documented and the other was denial. The mechanic is the argument.

## Why it worked
Truth telling (P13): the documentation speaks for itself when presented without editorial framing. The social format gave the argument native context — this was the platform where the "he said, she said" framing was most often deployed. No long form needed; the side-by-side is enough.

## Steal
When the argument can be made through simple juxtaposition of documented claim versus rebuttal, the most elegant format is the side-by-side. Do not editorialise what the evidence says for you.
