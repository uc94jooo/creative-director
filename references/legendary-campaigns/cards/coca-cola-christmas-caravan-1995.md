# Christmas Caravan (Coca-Cola · 1995)

## Insight

Coca-Cola had been associating itself with Christmas since the 1930s — it was Haddon Sundblom's Coca-Cola Santa illustrations that helped solidify the modern image of Santa Claus in red. By 1995, the brand could claim Christmas as genuine cultural territory rather than borrowed relevance. The Caravan is not an ad for Coke; it is the annual arrival of something people actually wait for.

## Mechanic

A TV film and subsequent real-world truck tour: illuminated red Coca-Cola trucks driving through snowy landscapes at night, with the tagline "Holidays Are Coming." The music became inseparable from the imagery. The trucks became a literal physical experience — the "Coca-Cola Christmas Truck Tour" visiting UK cities annually. Audiences queued hours to see the trucks.

## Why it worked

P10 (serialization and ritual): the Caravan created a genuine calendar ritual — audiences began treating the TV airing as the unofficial start of Christmas. P16 (long-form drama): the film's slow, cinematic movement built anticipation through atmosphere rather than message. The campaign proved that a brand can own a cultural moment so completely that the moment is incomplete without the brand's presence.

## Steal

If a brand has years of consistent seasonal presence, the goal shifts from "getting attention" to "activating an established ritual." The audience's anticipation does more work than any creative element. Build for the ritual first; content is secondary.
