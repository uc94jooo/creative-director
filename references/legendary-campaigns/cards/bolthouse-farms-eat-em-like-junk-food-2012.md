# Eat 'Em Like Junk Food (Bolthouse Farms · 2012)

## Insight
Healthy food marketing was uniformly earnest — salads in sunlight, athletes, green backgrounds. Children and adults chose junk food not because they didn't know vegetables were healthier, but because junk food was marketed as fun and vegetables as duty. The problem was not nutrition knowledge; it was desire engineering.

## Mechanic
Bolthouse Farms repackaged baby carrots using junk food codes: aggressive typography, dark packaging, machine-dispensed vending, bold flavor names like "ranch dipped." Carrots were placed in vending machines next to chips in school cafeterias. School trials showed carrot sales increased 97% in vending machines using junk food design versus standard packaging. The campaign extended to TV, social, and a partnership with schools.

## Why it worked
The insight was that children's behavior is primarily driven by desire, not information. Rather than increasing health education, Bolthouse changed the desire signal — packaging that looked like a product you'd want to grab impulsively. P03 (behavior inversion): every convention of health food marketing was inverted. P09: teachers and parents became co-participants in the vending machine pilots. The Effie win validated the sales result as genuine, not just PR.

## Steal
When rational argument has failed to change behavior, change the desire signal rather than the argument. Apply the emotional and aesthetic codes of the behavior you want to replace. People don't choose chips over carrots because chips taste better — they choose them because chips are marketed as something desirable.
