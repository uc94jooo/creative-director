# Trillion Dollar Campaign (The Zimbabwean · 2008)

## Insight
Zimbabwe's hyperinflation had rendered its currency literally worthless — $100 trillion notes existed but could not buy bread. The Zimbabwean newspaper, exiled in South Africa and banned in Zimbabwe, faced crippling import taxes designed to silence it. The insight: the evidence of the Mugabe government's economic destruction was physical — you could hold it in your hand.

## Mechanic
The agency printed outdoor posters and newspaper ads using genuine Zimbabwean billion- and trillion-dollar banknotes as the paper and ink. The banknotes cost less than the paper they'd be printed on if purchased conventionally. A billboard that was literally made of money — worthless money — with the headline "It's cheaper to print this on money than paper." P17 (design as idea): the medium was the entire argument. P13 (truth-telling): the economic reality of hyperinflation was made physically undeniable. P04 (brand as activist): a newspaper fighting for its survival used its own oppression as the story.

## Why it worked
There is no separation between the idea and the execution: the Zimbabwean banknotes were not a reference to hyperinflation, they were hyperinflation. The campaign cost nothing because the currency was worthless. The international media attention generated more press coverage than the newspaper could have purchased. Cannes Grand Prix and D&AD Black Pencil recognized a genuinely historic creative solution — using the problem as the medium. One of the most cited examples in advertising history of design doing the full argument.

## Steal
When the evidence of an injustice is physical, use the physical evidence as the medium. Don't describe the problem — embody it. The most powerful proof of an argument is when the argument is made of the problem itself.
