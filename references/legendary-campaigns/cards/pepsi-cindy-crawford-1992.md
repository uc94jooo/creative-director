# Cindy Crawford (Pepsi · 1992)

## Insight

Cindy Crawford in 1992 was the world's most famous supermodel. Two young boys watching her walk to a vending machine are not watching a model — they are watching the most desirable person they could imagine. But then she drinks the Pepsi. The boys' expressions make clear: they are admiring the can, not the woman. The joke inverts the expected sexual economy of celebrity advertising.

## Mechanic

A Super Bowl TV spot: two young boys sitting on a fence outside a country store watch Cindy Crawford arrive in a car, walk to a Pepsi machine, and drink a Pepsi in slow motion. Slow motion, iconic entrance, typically aspirational direction. Then the reveal: cut to the boys' faces, one saying "Is that a great car or what?" The other: admiring the can. The spot subverted celebrity endorsement by making the joke at its own expense.

## Why it worked

P16 (long-form drama) used the full build of classic celebrity entrance — slow motion, music, admiring gaze — to set up the inversion. The spot was self-aware about its own mechanisms: it knew the audience expected a certain kind of male gaze and subverted it for comedy. The irony communicated brand confidence — Pepsi was secure enough to make fun of its own marketing conventions. The execution became a touchstone for Super Bowl advertising craft.

## Steal

If you're going to use the conventions of aspirational celebrity advertising, go all the way in — then subvert them in the final beat. The subversion requires the full investment in the convention to earn its payoff. Half-measures make it merely confusing.
