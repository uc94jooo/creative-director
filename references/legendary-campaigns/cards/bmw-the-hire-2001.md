# The Hire (BMW · 2001)

## Insight
In 2001, the internet was fast enough to stream short video but brands still treated it as a brochure medium. BMW's potential buyers were affluent, digitally sophisticated, and had already rejected passive TV advertising. The insight: this audience would seek out entertainment if it was genuinely good enough — and BMW could make it.

## Mechanic
A series of eight short action films, each directed by a world-class filmmaker (Ang Lee, John Frankenheimer, Guy Ritchie, Tony Scott, John Woo, among others) and starring Clive Owen as "The Driver." Each film was a standalone cinematic experience featuring a BMW as the central object. Available only to download from bmwfilms.com. No TV distribution. Total views exceeded 100 million within the first year.

## Why it worked
BMW Films invented the branded content model a decade before it became a category. P18 (tech as canvas): the internet was the medium, not just the distribution channel — the exclusivity of online-only distribution was itself a brand signal of modernity and sophistication. P16 (long-form drama): each film had genuine narrative arc, A-list talent, and cinematic quality that respected the audience's intelligence. The car appeared as a character, not as a product. The BMW could be seen doing things it couldn't do in a 30-second spot. The quality signal was clear: BMW spent feature-film money on advertising — that spending transferred to product perception.

## Steal
When your audience will actively reject traditional advertising but will actively seek out entertainment, make entertainment that only works as it is. BMW Films couldn't have been broadcast — its power came from being sought. Seek-out changes the audience relationship from interruption to invitation.
