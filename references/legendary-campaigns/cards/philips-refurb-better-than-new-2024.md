# Refurb: Better Than New (Philips · 2024)

## Insight
Ten million holiday gifts are returned every year and thrown away — not because they are broken, but because making something new is cheaper than re-checking, repacking, and reshipping a returned item. A brand that builds products designed to last is selling against the very economic logic that destroys what it makes. The only credible response is to flip the e-commerce model itself.

## Mechanic
For one holiday season, Philips inverted its e-commerce model: instead of selling new products, the Philips Global website sold exclusively returned and refurbished products, at lower prices, with an upgraded warranty. An AR installation demonstrated the scale of holiday gift waste in real time, showing the physical accumulation of discarded returns, and directed viewers to the refurbished shop. The initiative sold out 52,000 refurbished products, prevented zero Philips products from reaching landfill that season, and avoided 185 tons of e-waste. Philips then made refurbished-first the default across its entire e-commerce ecosystem going forward.

## Why it worked
P10 (Radical Transparency): Philips exposed a systemic failure in its own industry — the return-and-trash model — and then did something about it rather than simply acknowledging it. P17 (Cause as Core): the brand claim "Designed for Life, Made to Last" was validated not by advertising but by restructuring the commercial model. The upgraded warranty on refurbished products reversed the usual consumer assumption (new = better) with a structural signal (we stand behind refurbished more, not less).

## Steal
When your brand promise is durability or quality, the strongest proof is to stake commercial value on things that have already been returned. Selling refurbished with a better warranty than new is more convincing than any durability claim in copy. Inverting your own commerce model for a season generates earned media that a campaign about the same principle cannot.
