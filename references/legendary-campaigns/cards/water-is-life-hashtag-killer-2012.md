# Hashtag Killer (Water is Life · 2012)

## Insight
The hashtag #FirstWorldProblems had become a social media ritual of performative self-deprecation — people posting trivial complaints ("My phone is almost dead and I can't find my charger") and tagging them to signal awareness of their own privilege. The ritual was simultaneously self-aware and self-serving: it acknowledged inequality without doing anything about it.

## Mechanic
Water is Life filmed people in Haiti — people living in genuine poverty, with real water scarcity — reading #FirstWorldProblems tweets straight to camera with deadpan sincerity. "I hate when my leather seats aren't heated." A Haitian man in a flooded area, reading it. The contrast was immediate and visceral. The campaign invited people to donate and hashtag #FirstWorldProblems in a new context — with a link to donate.

## Why it worked
P05 cultural hijack meets P04 activist mechanic: the campaign didn't create a new hashtag — it commandeered an existing viral behavior and redirected it. The humor of the original hashtag was turned into shame by the contrast, but shame that was immediately actionable (donate). The film was a model of economy: two elements (tweet text + Haitian context) needed no additional copy. P09: participants in the original hashtag became unwitting participants in the campaign.

## Steal
Find a viral behavior that contains a tension between what it signals (self-awareness) and what it does (nothing). Redirect the behavior toward the action it claims to support. The reframing is most powerful when the original participants immediately feel the contrast.
