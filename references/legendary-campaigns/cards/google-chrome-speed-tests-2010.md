# Speed Tests (Google Chrome · 2010)

## Insight
Browser speed was Chrome's primary competitive advantage over Firefox and IE — but "fast browser" is an abstract claim no one can feel. The challenge: make speed tangible, visual, and undeniable without resorting to spec sheets.

## Mechanic
A series of films testing Chrome's speed against physically fast phenomena: a potato gun, sound, and lightning. Each test was engineered as a genuine scientific demonstration, shot with high-speed cameras. The absurdist comparison (P11) made the demonstrations memorable; the product-as-proof mechanic (P14) made them credible. Chrome consistently matched or beat the comparison object.

## Why it worked
Product demonstration at its most inventive: instead of claiming speed, Chrome demonstrated it against referents the audience could visualize. The potato gun and lightning tests were genuinely engineered — this was not CGI. P14 worked because the demonstrations were physically real, not simulated. P11 elevated them from technical videos to culturally shareable content. The result: Chrome's speed advantage became memorable through absurdist physicality rather than spec comparison.

## Steal
When a product attribute is abstract (speed, power, protection), find a physical analogy that makes it visceral. Engineer the demonstration so it cannot be dismissed as trickery — the physicality of the proof is itself the brand message.
