# Frogs (Budweiser · 1995)

## Insight

The Budweiser name itself is a warm, low-register sonic experience — the bass frequencies in the word mirror the easy camaraderie of a cold beer with friends. A Super Bowl spot needed to communicate this through pure sound and rhythm rather than through a human story.

## Mechanic

A Super Bowl TV spot: three animatronic frogs in a swamp at night, each croaking one syllable — "Bud," "Weis," "Er" — in sequence, spelling out the brand name. No dialogue, no product shot for most of the film, no humans. Just the sound of a summer night and three frogs completing each other's sentences. The spot debuted at Super Bowl XXIX and immediately became one of the most discussed ads of the decade. Sequels followed with ferrets and iguanas.

## Why it worked

P11 (absurd as carrier): the premise has no logical connection to beer — and that's precisely why it works. The absurdity creates intrigue and warmth simultaneously. P16 (long-form drama) used restraint (almost nothing happens) to make the audience lean in. The sound design was the idea: the frogs' croaking was the brand's sonic equivalent — low, warm, and unhurried. A platform that spawned years of content without requiring a single product claim.

## Steal

When the brand name itself has sonic qualities worth communicating, find a vehicle that is entirely about sound rather than image. The less the brand appears visually, the more attention the audience pays to how it sounds.
