# Real Beauty Sketches (Dove · 2013)

## Insight
Women are their own harshest critics. When describing themselves to a stranger, they emphasize their perceived flaws — "my jaw is too wide," "I have a big forehead" — while others describe the same women with warmth and accuracy. The gap between self-perception and how others see you is a measurable, demonstrable truth about how women relate to their own appearance.

## Mechanic
An FBI-trained forensic artist sits behind a curtain. Women describe themselves; he draws from description only. Then a stranger who met the woman briefly describes the same woman; he draws again. The two portraits are placed side by side. The self-described portrait is always harsher, tighter, less open. The stranger's portrait is closer to beauty. Women see the difference and cry. 114 million YouTube views in the first week — the most watched branded video in history at the time.

## Why it worked
P12 (social experiment): the forensic artist creates genuine third-party credibility — this is not Dove telling women they are more beautiful than they think; this is an FBI-trained professional and a stranger demonstrating it. P03 (behavior inversion): beauty advertising shows external beauty; this reveals internal distortion. The emotional moment is earned through a structured experiment, not scripted drama. Cannes Grand Prix. Effie Grand Prix.

## Steal
Design the experiment so that the result could plausibly go the other way. Real Beauty Sketches works because the women might have described themselves accurately, or even generously — the fact that they do not is what makes the finding meaningful rather than manufactured. Credibility requires genuine risk of a different outcome.
