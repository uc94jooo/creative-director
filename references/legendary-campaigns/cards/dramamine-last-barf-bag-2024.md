# The Last Barf Bag (Dramamine · 2024)

## Insight
Dramamine works so well it is effectively killing the barf bag — and barf bag collectors, a small but devoted community of over 7,500 specimens, are facing extinction because of it. The brand's greatest product success is simultaneously its most absurd cultural crime. Admitting this with complete sincerity is funnier and more memorable than any product benefit claim.

## Mechanic
To celebrate its 75th anniversary, Dramamine created the world's first Barf Bag Museum — collecting over 7,500 barf bags from around the world and staging a legitimate gallery exhibition complete with curatorial notes, collectors on record, and solemn tributes to the bags Dramamine had rendered unnecessary. The campaign leaned fully into the absurd premise: if the product works, someone has to mourn what it destroys. Press coverage and social media treated the museum as genuine cultural news.

## Why it worked
P10 (Radical Transparency): the brand admitted its own product is "killing" an entire artifact category — a confession that disarms skepticism and produces delight. P11 (Absurd as Carrier): the barf bag museum is a perfectly absurd vehicle for a serious 75th anniversary message. The campaign avoided the trap of self-congratulatory milestone advertising by finding the strangest possible angle on longevity. Barf bag collectors are a real community; grounding the absurdity in genuine human passion gave the stunt credibility and earned media coverage in mainstream press.

## Steal
Find the thing your product destroys or replaces, then eulogize it publicly with full sincerity. The product's victim becomes the campaign's protagonist. This works best when the "victim" is obscure enough to be surprising but real enough to be credible — the gap between the seriousness of the tribute and the absurdity of the subject creates the comedy.
