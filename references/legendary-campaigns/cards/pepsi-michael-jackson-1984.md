# Michael Jackson (Pepsi · 1984)

## Insight

Michael Jackson in 1984 was the most culturally powerful individual on the planet — "Thriller" had just become the best-selling album in history. His endorsement was not a celebrity deal; it was a cultural merger. Pepsi buying Jackson meant Pepsi buying the zeitgeist.

## Mechanic

Two TV commercials featuring Michael Jackson performing "Billie Jean" and "Thriller"-era music — children mimicking him, then Jackson appearing. Shot at feature-film production standards. The deal was unprecedented: $5 million, artist control of music rights, significant creative input from Jackson himself. During filming, pyrotechnics accidentally set Jackson's hair on fire — the incident became a major news story, generating additional media coverage worth many times the advertising budget.

## Why it worked

P16 (long-form drama): the spots were produced as short films rather than commercials, with genuine cinematic quality that matched Jackson's artistic standards. The campaign demonstrated what happens when a brand secures not just a celebrity's image but their genuine creative participation — the result is qualitatively different from standard endorsement. The incident during filming also provided a case study in crisis communication that reinforced rather than damaged the partnership.

## Steal

When pursuing a major cultural figure for brand partnership, seek genuine creative involvement rather than image licensing. The difference between a celebrity using your product and a celebrity making something with your brand is the difference between an ad and a cultural event.
