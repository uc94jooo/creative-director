# Lizards (Budweiser · 1998)

## Insight

The Budweiser Frogs had become cultural icons — which meant a rival needed to exist within the same universe. The lizards (Frank and Louie) gave the campaign a villain energy: resentful, scheming, relatable in their jealousy. This is the natural expansion of a fictional world — you don't need to create new characters from scratch when you can create credible antagonists.

## Mechanic

A sequel campaign featuring two lizards — Frank and Louie — who resent the fame of the Budweiser frogs and scheme to replace them. The lizards' jealousy becomes a sustained comic storyline across multiple Super Bowl spots. Frank (voiced with deadpan menace) plotted against the frogs while Louie was his hapless accomplice. The characters became as beloved as the frogs they envied.

## Why it worked

P11 (absurd as carrier): expanding a fictional universe by adding antagonists creates a richer, more watchable world without abandoning the premise. The lizards' human-style ego and resentment was the source of all comedy. Budweiser proved that absurdist characters could sustain a multi-year narrative arc — something usually reserved for serialized TV content.

## Steal

Once you've established a successful absurdist character, the fastest way to generate new energy is to create a credible adversary with contrasting personality. The conflict generates story; the story generates audience investment without needing a new concept.
