# I Will What I Want (Under Armour · 2014)

## Insight
Female athletes are told — by experts, by culture, by their own families — what their bodies can and cannot do. The women who achieve despite this are not succeeding because of encouragement; they are succeeding in spite of rejection. That defiance is the product.

## Mechanic
Hero film featuring Misty Copeland, who was told at 13 she had the wrong body for ballet, performing the most physically demanding ballet she can — set against a rejection letter read aloud in voiceover. Extended across other athletes who rejected prescribed limits. Campaign drove a 28% increase in Under Armour women's line.

## Why it worked
Long-form drama (P16): the time needed to show the physical discipline and the emotional weight of the rejection letter simultaneously is earned by the film's structure. Truth telling (P13): Copeland's letter was real; her achievement was undeniable. The pairing of documented rejection with visible triumph is irrefutable. Vulnerable defiance is the tier-3 emotion — the strength comes from the admission of the obstacle, not its erasure.

## Steal
The most powerful female empowerment ad does not celebrate women as already powerful — it shows them becoming powerful despite specific named obstacles. The rejection letter is more effective than the medal. Specificity of the obstacle = universality of the aspiration.
