# Evan (Sandy Hook Promise · 2016)

## Insight
The warning signs of school violence are almost always visible beforehand — but people don't know what they look like, or don't register them as significant because their attention is on something else. The audience for gun violence prevention advertising is the same audience that was already watching.

## Mechanic
A film that opens as a sweet high school romance between Evan and a mystery girl — messages written on a library desk, clues left for each other. The audience watches the love story develop over several minutes. In the final thirty seconds, the camera re-frames: the desk was also being used by another student who was leaving the actual warning signs of planned violence, visible the whole time. The audience didn't see them because they were watching the romance.

## Why it worked
The film doesn't tell the audience they might miss warning signs — it makes them experience missing warning signs. The behaviour inversion (P03) is structural: what looked like a feel-good teen romance becomes evidence of collective inattention. The truth-telling (P13) is in the "you too" implication of the final reveal: the viewer is not a bystander — they were a participant.

## Steal
Design a film in which the audience's own inattention becomes the message. They don't need to be told they miss things — if they missed the same thing in your film, the point is made more powerfully than any instruction.
