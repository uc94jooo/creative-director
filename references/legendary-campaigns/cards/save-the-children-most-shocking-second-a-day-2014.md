# Most Shocking Second a Day (Save the Children · 2014)

## Insight
Syria is far away; a British child is close. The distance makes donors feel the crisis doesn't belong to them — proximity makes it impossible to ignore.

## Mechanic
A film constructed from one second of footage per day of a young British girl's life: birthday party, school, friends — then, as conflict arrives in Britain, one second per day as the world collapses around her, mirroring what Syrian children experience daily.

## Why it worked
The "second a day" format is native to the audience's social habits (a familiar creative trend at the time). Casting a relatable British child as the Syrian proxy forces empathetic projection. The structural inversion — familiar beginning, catastrophic ending — delivers impact through contrast rather than shock footage.

## Steal
Transplant a distant tragedy into the local audience's world using a single conditional proxy character. Empathy scales when the audience can see themselves in the victim.
