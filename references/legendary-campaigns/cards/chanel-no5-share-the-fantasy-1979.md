# Share the Fantasy (Chanel No. 5 · 1979)

## Insight

Luxury fragrance is not about smell — it is about the self you believe you could inhabit. Chanel No. 5's insight was that its aspiration was not realistic or grounded; it was explicitly a fantasy — and rather than hiding this, the brand should celebrate and share it.

## Mechanic

A 30-second TV film directed by Ridley Scott: a woman in a white dress by a pool, a man diving in, surreal editing, a dreamlike visual language with no narrative resolution. The film is closer to an art object than a commercial — associative, non-linear, unresolved. No product claims. The bottle appears at the end. Scott's direction was so cinematic it read as short film rather than advertising.

## Why it worked

P16 (long-form drama): by borrowing the visual language of European art cinema, the film transferred that cultural capital to the brand. Chanel wasn't making an ad — it was making a visual tone poem that happened to end with the product. The unresolved narrative was a feature, not a bug: the audience was invited to complete the fantasy themselves, making the brand's territory infinitely personal.

## Steal

For luxury brands, aspiration is not served by explanation. The more precisely you describe the fantasy, the less powerful it becomes. Withhold resolution. Let the audience project themselves into the incomplete image — their version of the fantasy is always more compelling than yours.
