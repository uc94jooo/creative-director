# Little Girls (Dove · 2007)

## Insight
Girls as young as five internalize negative messages about their bodies from the images and narratives they see around them. They are not born with body image issues — they acquire them from culture. A brand that claims to fight for real beauty should acknowledge this acquisition at its earliest stage.

## Mechanic
A film showing young girls — barely old enough to read — describing their bodies in the language of adult female self-criticism: "My tummy is too big." "I hate my hair." "I wish I were prettier." The camera holds on their faces — innocent, utterly genuine. The effect is profoundly uncomfortable. The film closes with the proposition that low self-esteem in young women begins here, at this age, and that we can change it.

## Why it worked
P13 (truth telling): the truth being told — that body image anxiety starts in childhood — was not being stated anywhere in the beauty category. Dove had the courage to name it directly. P03 (behavior inversion): beauty advertising normally aspires; this confronts. The discomfort is not accidental — it is the mechanism by which the message is delivered and retained.

## Steal
When your brand's purpose intersects with a truth about children's experience, the stakes rise automatically. Audiences cannot dismiss discomfort about children the same way they dismiss discomfort about adults. The emotional weight is inherently amplified.
