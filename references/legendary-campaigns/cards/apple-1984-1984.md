# 1984 (Apple · 1984)

## Insight
IBM's dominance over personal computing was not just a market position — it was an ideology: technology as uniformity, conformity, corporate control. Apple's Macintosh was not just a better computer; it was a democratic act against that ideology. The insight: the only way to announce this product was as an act of liberation.

## Mechanic
A 60-second film directed by Ridley Scott, aired once on national television during Super Bowl XVIII on January 22, 1984, and never broadcast again. Grey-clad drones march toward a screen showing Big Brother delivering a propaganda speech. A woman in athletic colours runs through the space and throws a hammer through the screen. Tagline: "On January 24th, Apple Computer will introduce Macintosh. And you'll see why 1984 won't be like 1984." No product shot. No demonstration. No price. One line of copy.

## Why it worked
P16 (long-form drama): in 60 seconds, Ridley Scott constructs a complete cinematic world — world-building, protagonist, antagonist, climax. P13 (truth telling): in 1984, IBM's dominance was a genuine cultural anxiety; the ad names it without softening. P06 (limitation as power): Apple had zero market share — positioning as the underdog challenger was not false modesty but strategic truth. The rarity of the broadcast (aired once) made it mythological. Named by Advertising Age as the greatest commercial of all time.

## Steal
A product launch does not require showing the product. It requires showing what the world would be without the product — and what the world will be because of it. The Macintosh appears for exactly zero seconds in the film that launched it.
