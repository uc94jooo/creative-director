# Alexander Hamilton (Got Milk · 1993)

## Insight
Deprivation is funnier when the stakes are highest. An Alexander Burr history obsessive with $10,000 riding on a phone-in trivia answer has the highest imaginable stakes for a man with peanut butter in his mouth and no milk in his refrigerator. The comedy of the spot comes from perfect proportionality: massive stakes, tiny physical obstacle, no milk.

## Mechanic
The original and most famous Got Milk? execution. A man alone in his house is eating peanut butter sandwiches while surrounded by Aaron Burr memorabilia. He wins a trivia call-in radio contest asking "who shot Alexander Hamilton?" — but cannot answer through the peanut butter. The radio host hangs up. He staggers to his empty refrigerator. Tagline: "Got milk?" 60-second TV spot. Created the Got Milk? campaign and the tagline used for over 20 years.

## Why it worked
P11 (absurd as carrier): the proportionality is the joke — the world's most specialized knowledge rendered useless by the world's most mundane problem. The film is perfectly constructed: the backstory (why he knows the answer), the escalation (the stakes of the call), and the deflation (the empty fridge) are all necessary. Remove any element and the spot fails. This execution is genuinely near-perfect in its construction.

## Steal
Absurdist comedy requires precision of cause and effect. The peanut butter and the trivia question are not arbitrary — they create the exact conditions under which milk's absence is maximally consequential. Identify the scenario where your product's absence has the most embarrassing or costly consequences for the most sympathetic person.
