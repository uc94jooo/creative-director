# The Misheard Version (Specsavers · 2024)

## Insight
People avoid getting a hearing test for up to ten years because they associate it with feeling old. But mishearing is genuinely funny — the misheard song lyric is a universal, joyful human experience that anyone can relate to without stigma. If a hearing test is disguised as a game of misheard lyrics, the barrier disappears because the activity no longer signals decline; it signals participation in a shared cultural joke.

## Mechanic
Specsavers partnered with Rick Astley to re-record his globally famous hit "Never Gonna Give You Up" using the most common internet-reported misheard lyrics in place of the real ones — effectively creating the world's first mass hearing test disguised as a novelty song. The track was released without explanation: no brand announcement, no instructions, no disclosure that it was an advertisement for hearing tests. It simply appeared as a strange new Rick Astley version. The public reacted with confusion, delight, and the social behavior of comparing what they heard. The song was played over 20 million times in its first 8 hours. Hearing loss searches increased 138%. Specsavers hearing test bookings increased 1,220% above target. Zero media spend. Rick Astley's career experienced another major cultural resurgence in the process.

## Why it worked
P01 (Format as Idea): a mass hearing test that functions as a piece of pop music is only possible because the format — the deliberately wrong lyric — IS the diagnostic tool. The song is the product. P06 (Earned Media Trap): releasing a bizarrely wrong version of one of the most-recognized songs in the world without explanation guarantees that every person who encounters it will share it to ask "what is happening." The confusion and delight are the media. The 1,220% booking uplift with zero media spend is the efficiency benchmark this pattern can achieve when concept and format are perfectly aligned.

## Steal
When your product addresses something people avoid because it feels stigmatizing, find the version of that experience that is funny rather than frightening — then make that the entry point. A hearing test you don't know you're taking beats a hearing test you've been avoiding for ten years. The insight is: remove the name of the thing, keep the function.
