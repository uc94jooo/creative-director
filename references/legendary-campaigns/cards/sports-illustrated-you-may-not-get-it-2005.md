# You May Not Get It (Sports Illustrated · 2005)

## Insight
Sports Illustrated is a magazine that understands sports culture on a level that casual observers cannot access — the inside knowledge, the history, the statistical literacy. Non-sports-readers couldn't understand the allure; devoted readers needed no explanation.

## Mechanic
Print and OOH campaign deploying absurdist, highly sports-specific humor designed to be completely opaque to non-fans and immediately hilarious to insiders. The tagline "You may not get it" was directed at non-fans — an inversion of the typical advertising impulse to broaden appeal. P17 (design as idea): the visual execution was spare, relying on typography and specific references rather than illustration or photography.

## Why it worked
By explicitly excluding non-fans, SI strengthened its position with the audience it actually served. "You may not get it" validated the insider identity of sports fans — their understanding of the references was a badge of belonging. The absurdism worked because it was specifically legible to sports fans who had the context; opaque absurdism would have failed.

## Steal
Exclusion can be a stronger loyalty signal than inclusion. When your audience has genuine insider knowledge, create work that rewards that knowledge and explicitly acknowledges that outsiders won't understand. The exclusion confirms that you are in the in-group.
