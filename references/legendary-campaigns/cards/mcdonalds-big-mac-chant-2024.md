# Big Mac Chant (McDonald's Taiwan · 2024)

## Insight

The Big Mac's original jingle — "two all-beef patties, special sauce, lettuce, cheese, pickles, onions, on a sesame seed bun" (雙層純牛肉、獨特醬料、生菜、起司、酸黃瓜、洋蔥，芝麻麵包蓋上去) — is a shared memory across two Taiwanese generations (GenX and GenY), but a straight rerun of that nostalgia only flatters the people who already remember it and locks everyone else out. Nostalgia broadcast one-way creates a generation gap, not a reunion.

## Mechanic

McDonald's Taiwan posted an open-ended Facebook comment starting with "雙層純牛肉…" and let fans finish the chant in the replies. Three response patterns emerged organically: die-hards who recited the jingle correctly, a large "almost remember it" middle group who filled the blank with punning mis-completions (獨特醬料加菲貓 / 洋蔥急事 / U2棉被蓋上去), and newcomers with zero memory of the original ad. The brand then took the best fan-submitted lines and had KOLs, KOCs, and everyday creators turn them into original songs across multiple genres (rap, acoustic folk, comedy filter videos, family sing-alongs), releasing the results as UGC videos and formally uploading tracks to StreetVoice (街聲), a Taiwanese music-streaming platform for independent artists — positioning the output as real songs, not branded content.

## Why it worked

User as Co-Author, stacked on a cultural hijack of the brand's own back catalog: instead of re-airing the old ad, the brand handed the unfinished line to the crowd and let imperfect memory become the creative material. The "wrong answers" (punning mis-completions) weren't noise to clean up — they were the funniest, most shareable output, because getting the chant "wrong" is a lower-pressure, more playful way to participate than reciting it correctly. Turning crowd-sourced comments into a real, multi-genre, streaming-platform-released song collapsed the distance between "brand campaign" and "genuine internet culture," which is why the results (2.24M+ streams, McDonald's Taiwan's best single-day FB engagement of the year) beat contemporary pop releases rather than just other fast-food social content.

## Steal

If a brand owns a piece of dormant IP that used to be collectively memorized (a jingle, a slogan, a mascot line), don't just replay it — open a fill-in-the-blank and let the crowd's imperfect recall generate the punchlines. Then invest real production value (multiple genres, real artists, real distribution) in turning the best crowd lines into something that can live outside the campaign, on a platform where it competes with real culture, not just other ads.
