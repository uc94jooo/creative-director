# Christmas is For Sharing (Sainsbury's · 2014)

## Insight
The 1914 Christmas Truce — when soldiers on both sides laid down arms, crossed no-man's land, and played football — is the most powerful evidence that humanity refuses to be destroyed by war. Sharing a bar of chocolate across enemy lines is the simplest and most devastating illustration of what Christmas actually means.

## Mechanic
A feature-quality period war film recreating the 1914 Christmas Truce, made in collaboration with the Royal British Legion. A German soldier and a British soldier share a chocolate bar in no-man's land. Real historical events, real characters (loosely), cinematographic quality. The Sainsbury's chocolate bar — a replica of a real 1914 wartime edition — was sold in stores, with all profits going to the Royal British Legion.

## Why it worked
Every decision was load-bearing: the historical moment (authentic), the collaboration (credible), the product connection (not forced — chocolate was in rations), the charity tie-in (meaning real cost to the brand), the production quality (respect, not nostalgia-wash). The result was the most-viewed Christmas ad in history at that point.

## Steal
If you're going to use a historical event in advertising, the minimum requirement is authentic collaboration with the institutions that own the memory. "Inspired by" is not enough. Sainsbury's worked with the Royal British Legion. That collaboration was the moral basis for the film.
