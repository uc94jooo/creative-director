# Hilltop (Coca-Cola · 1971)

## Insight

In 1971, America was fractured by Vietnam, race, and generation. Coca-Cola wanted to say something about peace and unity — but every brand trying that looked cynical. The insight: don't make a statement about the world, make a picture of the world you wish existed, and put it on a hill in Italy with real young people from every nation singing together.

## Mechanic

A TV film directed by Harvey Gabor: a multicultural group of young people on a hilltop in Manziana, Italy, singing "I'd Like to Buy the World a Coke" (written by Bill Backer at McCann). 200 young people from 30+ countries. Shot in Europe; the song became a hit when released as a standalone record. Coca-Cola received 100,000 letters asking where they could buy the song. It was re-released as a pop single by The New Seekers.

## Why it worked

P07 (invisible brand): the brand appears in the jingle and a final product shot, but 95% of the film is a vision of human harmony that exists independently of the product. The audience doesn't feel sold to — they feel invited. P16 (long-form drama) gave the song time to build into a genuine emotional experience. P10 (serialization and ritual): "Hilltop" established a template for Coke as the brand of moments of universal human connection, a territory occupied for decades afterward.

## Steal

In a divided cultural moment, the brand that pictures unity rather than comments on it earns the emotional territory. Don't address the conflict — embody its resolution. The image must be vivid enough that people want to live in it.
