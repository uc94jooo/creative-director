# Think Small (Volkswagen · 1959)

## Insight
Post-war American culture worshipped size — big cars, big houses, big portions. The Volkswagen Beetle was objectively small, slow, and underpowered. Every American car advertiser showed their car large, gleaming, and lifestyle-aspirational. The insight: honesty about smallness is more powerful than the lie of bigness, because every consumer already knows the Beetle is small.

## Mechanic
A print advertisement designed by Helmut Krone, with copy by Julian Koenig. A tiny Volkswagen Beetle floats in the upper left of an otherwise white page — surrounded by empty space that emphasizes its smallness. Below, in small type: "Think small." The body copy lists the Beetle's actual limitations with complete honesty, framing each as an advantage. No lifestyle photography. No beautiful people. No aspirational setting. Just a small car, a lot of white space, and an invitation to reconsider what you want from a car.

## Why it worked
P17 (design as idea): Krone's white space is the advertisement — the abundance of nothing around the car makes smallness a design statement. P06 (limitation as power): every supposed weakness of the Beetle is listed and reframed without apology. P07 (invisible brand): the Beetle is so small in the page that it practically disappears — the car's modesty is the argument. Named the greatest print ad of the 20th century by Advertising Age. Credited with inventing modern advertising as a discipline of ideas rather than decoration.

## Steal
When your product is genuinely inferior to the competition on the category's dominant axis, do not compete on that axis. Redefine what the axis should be. "Think Small" does not argue that small is as good as big — it argues that small is a different and better choice for a different and better kind of person.
